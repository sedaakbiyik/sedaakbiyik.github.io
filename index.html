Hi. I'm Seda Karakose-Akbiyik, a PhD Candidate at Harvard University Department of Psychology, where I work under the guidance of Professor Alfonso Caramazza. My earlier academic journey includes an Master of Science in Cognitive Psychology from Koç University, where I was advised by Professors Fuat Balci and Tilbe Göksun, and a BA in Psychology from Boğaziçi University.

The human mind is an extraordinary network, as complex in its own way as the world around us. Within this network, a multitude of cognitive processes occur: recognizing objects, making sense of actions, and comprehending language, among others. But how do different regions of the brain contribute to these complex tasks? What are the cognitive mechanisms underlying these processes?

My research focuses on unraveling this, probing the functional organization of the mind and brain. I focus on how different brain regions and networks contribute to a range of cognitive operations, including object and action recognition, conceptual knowledge representation, and language processing. I employ functional neuroimaging and other cognitive science methodologies (e.g. behavioral studies, computational modeling, neuromodulation) to delve deeper into the functional properties of different brain regions, aiming to elucidate how they encode information and support cognitive functions.

In addition to working with neurotypical individuals, my research extends to special populations, such as those born blind. These cases yield important insights into how sensory experiences—or the lack thereof—influence the brain's functional organization. Investigations into individuals who have experienced brain damage also form a key part of my research. Such cases serve as 'experiments of nature,' facilitating invaluable inferences about the typical organization and functioning of the human mind.
